What We Heard

You run after-school and summer programming for about 120 kids across three Detroit neighborhoods, and your days split roughly in half: half actually running programs, half managing the paper trail that keeps them funded. That paper trail lives in a lot of places at once — Google Docs for grant reports and board updates, Excel for tracking attendance and outcomes data, Mailchimp for parent communications, and a shared Drive folder that has become, in your words, “a graveyard of old versions.” You write a lot. Quarterly reports to three different funders, each with their own format and their own definition of what an outcome looks like. Monthly parent newsletters. Board updates before every meeting. You estimate you spend somewhere between eight and twelve hours a week just on writing and formatting, and most of it feels like the same information reorganized slightly differently for a different audience.

What has stopped you from doing more with AI is a specific concern, not a vague one: you have participant data. Names, ages, attendance records, some case notes. You are not putting that into a chatbot. That boundary is firm and it should be — but it has also kept you from touching AI for anything, even the work that has nothing sensitive in it at all. The writing. The formatting. The reorganization of information you already have. You have been leaving time on the table on the assumption that AI is all-or-nothing, when really the sensitive stuff and the administrative stuff are separable.

What success looks like for you is not complicated: fewer Sunday nights writing the same grant report for the fourth time in slightly different language. More time on program design, staff support, and the relationships that actually move the work forward.

What We Suggest

The right system for you has two distinct layers, and it is important to keep them separate — not just for your comfort, but because it is actually the right architecture. Layer one is a private, document-based AI workspace where you store your program information, your voice, your funder templates, and your outcome frameworks. Nothing with participant data lives here. This layer handles all your writing and formatting work. Layer two — your case management and attendance tracking — stays exactly where it is, untouched by AI, until you have a specific reason and a proper evaluation process to change that.

For layer one, Claude Projects is the right starting point. A Claude Project is a private workspace — nothing leaves it, nothing is used to train models, and you control exactly what goes in. You build one project for your program communications work and upload your existing assets: a strong grant report you have already written, your current outcome definitions, your funder requirements documents, your org boilerplate. From that point forward, when you need to write a quarterly report, you are not starting from scratch. You are asking Claude to draft a report for Funder A using the outcomes data you paste in — no names, no participant records, just aggregate numbers — and it produces a draft in Funder A’s format because that template is already in the project. You edit, you submit. The estimate from people doing this in similar roles is two to three hours of writing work replaced by thirty minutes of review and editing.

For parent communications, Mailchimp’s built-in AI tools can handle first drafts of your newsletters given a few bullet points about what happened that month. This does not require any new tools or any participant data — just the program-level updates you are already writing in some form. The first move, though, is not Mailchimp. It is the Claude Project. Set that up with your three best existing reports and your outcome framework document, and run your next quarterly report through it before you do anything else. One report. See what it saves you. Then expand.

If you build this system and actually use it, Sunday nights look different. The quarterly report for the Wallace Foundation does not take four hours — it takes forty-five minutes because the structure is already right, the language is already calibrated, and you are editing rather than generating. That time goes somewhere else. It goes toward the site visit preparation you never have enough time for, or the new curriculum you have been meaning to develop for the past two years, or just leaving work before 7pm on a weekday.

Quick Wins
a few hours, no new tools
▼

Use what you already have differently. Start here if you want to see real time savings before committing to anything new.

1
Open Claude.ai and create a free account if you don’t have one. Go to Projects and create one called something like “Program Communications.”
2
Upload two or three of your strongest existing grant reports and your current outcomes framework document into the project. These become Claude’s reference for your voice, your programs, and your funder expectations.
3
The next time you need to write a quarterly report, open that project, paste in your aggregate outcomes data (no participant names or records — just numbers and program-level notes), and ask Claude to draft a report in the format of whichever funder you’re writing for. Edit the draft. Compare the time to your usual process.
Off-the-Shelf Stack
a weekend, no code
▼

Build a proper communications system. The right level if you want something that materially changes your weekly workload.

1
Upgrade to Claude Pro ($20/month) for higher usage limits and better performance on longer documents. Add all your funder templates, boilerplate language, program descriptions, and board report formats to your Claude Project.
2
Create a simple “outputs” folder in Google Drive for Claude-drafted documents. Establish a workflow: outcomes data goes in, draft comes out, you edit in Google Docs, final goes to funder. One clean path, every time.
3
Set up Mailchimp’s AI writing assistant for your parent newsletter. Create a monthly template with your program sections already defined. Each month, fill in the bullet points of what happened, let the AI draft the narrative, edit for your voice, send.
Connected System
ongoing, some setup time
▼

Automate the repetitive parts so the writing work nearly disappears.

1
Build a proper Google Drive filing system that separates participant records (stays locked down) from program content and communications assets (available to AI tools). This architecture is the foundation — get it right before connecting anything.
2
Use Zapier or Make to connect your outcomes tracking spreadsheet to a Claude-powered drafting workflow. When you update your monthly outcomes data, a draft report section auto-generates into a Google Doc for your review. You edit and approve; you don’t generate from scratch.
3
Explore Grantable or Instrumentl for grant research and first-draft generation. These tools are built specifically for nonprofits, understand funder language, and keep your data private. Layer them on top of your existing Claude Project workflow once that foundation is solid.
