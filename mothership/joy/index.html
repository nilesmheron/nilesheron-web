Mothership / Cover Letter
1420 Washington · Detroit · 2026

I've spent the last several days thinking about The Arkestra, and The Mothership. Why they were necessary. What galaxy they came from.

I've been building something — or trying to — at the intersection of innovation, equity, and Detroit. And somewhere in the middle of that work, I found myself needing to understand what tradition we were actually building inside of. Not the institutional version. Not the academic version. The real one.

As far as I can tell, Sun Ra's Arkestra and George Clinton's Mothership are for their creators what Wu-Tang's Shaolin Temple was: an explanation of how joy and hope and creativity can come from the same people who had their names and cultures stripped from them. All of these origin stories are trying to answer the same question. I had just never noticed that before.

What surprised me most was the gospel. I had never considered that Wu-Tang and Fred Hammond and Beyoncé and — personal feelings about the artist notwithstanding — Kanye West are all fundamentally Black liberationists. Standing in the same lineage. Descended from George Clinton and Octavia Butler and the choir directors at hush harbor churches whose names we will never know.

In a lot of ways I still have more questions than answers. What I wrote is an attempt to name what I think we are building, and why. It's for the people making decisions about a building and a committee and a brief for architects. And it's for the founders we haven't met yet — who will walk through those doors in a few years not knowing why the space feels like it was made for them, but feeling it anyway.

It's a playlist, and a manifesto, and a plea.
